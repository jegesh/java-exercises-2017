data-reactid="153"><!-- react-text: 154 -->With <!-- /react-text --><span data-page="page_1" data-reactid="155"></span><!-- react-text: 156 -->the tacit support of President Trump, King Salman of
Saudi Arabia and his powerful son launched an unprecedented purge of
their own family over the weekend. The major targets were royal brethren
who controlled money, the media, or the military. Among the dozens
arrested were eleven senior princes, several current or former
ministers, the owners of three major television stations, the head of
the most important military branch, and one of the wealthiest men in the
world, who has been a major shareholder in Citibank, Twentieth Century
Fox, Apple, Twitter, and Lyft.<!-- /react-text --></p><p data-reactid="157"><!-- react-text: 158 -->“It’s the equivalent of waking up to find Warren Buffett and the heads
of ABC, CBS and NBC have been arrested,” a former U.S. official told me.
“It has all the appearances of a coup d’état. Saudi Arabia is rapidly
becoming another country. The kingdom has never been this unstable.”<!-- /react-text --></p><p data-reactid="159"><!-- react-text: 160 -->The purge sent shockwaves of fear through the kingdom—one of the world’s
two largest producers and exporters of oil—as well as the Middle East,
global financial markets, and the international community. The arrests
<!-- /react-text --><a href="http://www.reuters.com/article/us-saudi-arrests/purge-of-saudi-princes-businessmen-widens-travel-curbs-imposed-idUSKBN1D60S6" target="_blank" class="ArticleBody__link___1FS03" data-reactid="161"><!-- react-text: 162 -->continued<!-- /react-text --></a><!-- react-text: 163 --> on Monday, with no indication when the crackdown might end.<!-- /react-text --></p><p data-reactid="164"><!-- react-text: 165 -->Both critics and supporters believe that the purge’s
mastermind is Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman, who has had a meteoric
rise since he was appointed by his father to be defense minister, at the
age of twenty-nine, in 2015. He has vowed to modernize the
ultra-conservative tribal society. But, to do it, he has grabbed all the
major economic, political, security, and royal court portfolios. In
June, he was instrumental in ousting the former Crown Prince—Prince
Nayef, a senior royal and the closest U.S. ally inside the monarchy—to
become next in line to the throne. (Nayef is still under house arrest,
according to Human Rights Watch.) In September, Crown Prince Mohammed
orchestrated the arrest of well-known intellectuals and clerics.<!-- /react-text --></p><p data-reactid="166"><!-- react-text: 167 -->On Saturday, King Salman created a new Anti-Corruption Commission and
put M.B.S.—as his third son is commonly known, by his initials—in
charge. The arrests, in a late-night blitz, soon followed.<!-- /react-text --></p><p data-reactid="168"><!-- react-text: 169 -->“It’s an interesting form of dictatorship that is being created in Saudi
Arabia,” Jamal Khashoggi, a prominent Saudi columnist and former editor

becoming the supreme leader.” The only other country where that title is
used is Iran, Saudi Arabia’s archrival.<!-- /react-text --></p><p data-reactid="170"><!-- react-text: 171 -->The arrests are an attempt to consolidate the Crown Prince’s
powers, possibly in the run-up to a move by the aged and ailing king to
step aside, experts say. The father-and-son duo have already created a
whole new royal family that bypassed hundreds (at least) of other
princes. “The ruling House of Saud and the entire world now knows that
Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman is ready to resort to any means to
consolidate his bid to take over upon the death or abdication of his
eighty-one-year-old father, King Salman,” David Ottaway, a fellow at the
Woodrow Wilson Center, in Washington, said in a statement e-mailed to
me. “Nothing like this has ever happened before in the history of Saudi
Arabia, giving the sense the kingdom is entering into unchartered waters
with unknown consequences.”<!-- /react-text --></p><p data-reactid="172"><!-- react-text: 173 -->The Crown Prince is also empowered to seize assets and issue travel
bans.
The <!-- /react-text --><em data-reactid="174"><!-- react-text: 175 -->Times<!-- /react-text --></em><!-- react-text: 176 --> <!-- /react-text --><a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2017/11/05/world/middleeast/saudi-crown-prince-purge.html?_r=0" target="_blank" class="ArticleBody__link___1FS03" data-reactid="177"><!-- react-text: 178 -->reported<!-- /react-text --></a><!-- react-text: 179 --> that
all members of the sprawling Saudi royal family were also barred from
leaving the country. Ibn Saud, the founding king of modern Saudi Arabia,
had more than forty sons and even more daughters. Their progeny are
now <!-- /react-text --><a href="https://www.forbes.com/2010/07/12/saudi-arabia-royalty-oil-opinions-columnists-ilan-berman.html" target="_blank" class="ArticleBody__link___1FS03" data-reactid="180"><!-- react-text: 181 -->estimated<!-- /react-text --></a><!-- react-text: 182 --> to
be anywhere from six thousand to fifteen thousand,
with <!-- /react-text --><em data-reactid="183"><!-- react-text: 184 -->Forbes<!-- /react-text --></em><!-- react-text: 185 --> suggesting, in 2010, that the number could be twice that
large.<!-- /react-text --></p><p data-reactid="186"><!-- react-text: 187 -->After Ibn Saud’s death, in 1953, the first generation of sons passed the
kingship down the line—with the consent of the other brothers. They
ruled by consensus. This is no longer the case: a young prince from
among the grandsons has now pushed all others aside.<!-- /react-text --></p><p data-reactid="188"><!-- react-text: 189 -->“What is striking is how this has been a methodical process. He’s taken

Security Council staffer in the Obama Administration, told me. “No one has been able to stop him.
He’s bested his opponents.”<!-- /react-text --></p><p data-reactid="190"><!-- react-text: 191 -->The Trump Administration supports the sweeping changes that
have redefined the kingdom—and the royal family—over the past two years.
En route to Asia, just hours before the purge on Saturday, the President
spoke with the king from Air Force One to praise him and the Crown
Prince for making statements on “the need to build a moderate, peaceful,
and tolerant region,” which is “essential to ensuring a hopeful future

radical ideology—once and for all—so the world can be safe from its
evil,” the White House reported in an unusually detailed statement.<!-- /react-text --></p><p data-reactid="192"><!-- react-text: 193 -->Trump also said that he is personally trying to convince the

shares in the U.S. but they<!-- /react-text --><span data-page="page_2" data-reactid="196"></span><!-- react-text: 197 --> include the prospect that any Saudi assets

Saud; Trump’s first foreign trip was to Saudi Arabia. Jared Kushner,
Trump’s son-in-law, made an <!-- /react-text --><a href="https://www.politico.com/story/2017/10/29/jared-kushner-saudi-arabia-244291" target="_blank" class="ArticleBody__link___1FS03" data-reactid="230"><!-- react-text: 231 -->unannounced

a <!-- /react-text --><a href="http://www.bbc.com/news/world-middle-east-41870406" target="_blank" class="ArticleBody__link___1FS03" data-reactid="251"><!-- react-text: 252 -->threat<!-- /react-text --></a><!-- react-text: 253 --> to his
